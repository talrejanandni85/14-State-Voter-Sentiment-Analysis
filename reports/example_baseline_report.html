Methodology

How This Survey Works

Respondents score opinions on a 0–100 scale. A score of 51 does not mean 51% support — it means the average strength-of-support was 51 out of 100. This captures intensity, not just direction.

Panel: An online longitudinal panel of registered voters across 14 priority battleground states. Respondents receive a personalized queue of ≤10 questions per session and may respond once per day. The same questions recur over time, enabling tracking of opinion shifts across years.

Weighting: All averages are weighted means using per-respondent weights that correct for demographic imbalances in the panel relative to the target voter population in each state.

Suppression: Subgroups with fewer than 30 respondents are suppressed and shown as **.

Deduplication: Where a respondent appears multiple times, only the most recent response is retained.

Key Takeaways

What the Data Shows

01 — Policy A Overall

Overall average of 49.9 sits just below the midpoint — a near-split electorate. Partisan sorting is near-total: Republicans average 84.7, Democrats 24.6 — a 60-point gap.

02 — Policy A Polarization

34.9% scored 0–19 (strong opposition) and 38.5% scored 80–100 (strong support). Just 18% landed in the 20–79 middle range. Bimodal distribution signals a deeply divided issue.

03 — Policy A Rising

Support has risen each year: 44.2 (2019) → 46.9 (2023) → 50.9 (2024) → 53.4 (2025 YTD). The largest jump happened 2023–2025, a +6.5 point shift over two years.

04 — Policy A Recent Dip

The last 2-month window shows a modest −2.2 point pullback: from 53.65 on Feb 10 to 51.49 on Apr 22, 2025 — possibly reflecting short-term news cycle effects.

05 — Policy B Support

Coalition-building statement averages 70.6 — strong net support. 56.0% scored 80–100 vs. only 11.5% at 0–19. Notably, Republicans still average 57.9 — cross-partisan support.

06 — Policy B Partisanship

Partisan pattern inverts from Policy A: Democrats lead at 85.0, Independents at 69.9, Republicans at 57.9. Race groups converge more tightly (69.7–78.5 range).

07 — Income Gap

On Policy A, over-$75k respondents average 54.4 vs. 49.0 under $75k. On Policy B the income gap narrows: under-$75k respondents average 75.6 vs. overall panel 70.6 — lower-income voters more supportive.

08 — Education & Area

Non-college voters average 54.9 on Policy A vs. college at 48.5. Rural voters average 57.5 vs. urban at 45.3 — a 12-point area gap, the largest subgroup divergence besides party.

Notes

Anonymization & Methodology Notes

Anonymization: All data has been anonymized for portfolio use. Respondent IDs replaced with synthetic UUIDs. Response values, weights, and ages lightly perturbed (±5%). Policy question text generalized. Directional relationships and analytical structure preserved throughout.

Suppression: Cells marked ** indicate subgroups with n<30, suppressed to prevent unreliable small-sample estimates. Dashes (–) indicate zero or near-zero respondents in that cell.

Weighting: All averages are weighted means using per-respondent weights provided in the panel export. Weights correct for demographic imbalances relative to the registered voter population in each state.

BIPOC: Calculated as the weighted mean across respondents who identified as African American, Native American, Latino/Hispanic, Asian, or Other.

Pipeline: All analysis performed in R using tidyverse, readxl, and writexl. Full processing script: analysis/baseline_survey_analysis.Rmd. Outputs exported as multi-sheet Excel files used directly in client PowerPoint reports.
